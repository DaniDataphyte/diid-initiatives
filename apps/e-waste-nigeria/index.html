Used electrical and electronic equipment enters through ports, traders, and markets before much of it becomes uneconomical to repair.

05 / REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

Nigeria has a regulatory framework for e-waste management, but enforcement remains the central failure point.

EXISTING INSTRUMENTS

2011

NESREA e-waste regulations

The primary domestic framework prohibits open burning and landfill disposal while requiring manufacturers and importers to comply with Extended Producer Responsibility.

1991 / 2004

Basel and Bamako commitments

Nigeria is part of the main international legal architecture governing hazardous waste flows, including bans and prior informed consent obligations.

2025

Basel e-waste amendments enter into force

From January 1, 2025, all transboundary e-waste and scrap shipments fall under prior informed consent requirements.

2026

NESREA cooperative-led model

The new model aims to integrate informal workers through structured cooperatives tied to governance, financing, compliance, and social protection support.

THE ENFORCEMENT GAP

Law exists, field conditions do not change at scale

Unsafe dismantling and burning continue because collection channels, recycling infrastructure, and enforcement reach remain weak.

Formal operators lose feedstock to informal markets

Informal recyclers and scavengers outcompete registered operators on price, leaving formal systems under-supplied.

Weak import controls

Customs data does not clearly distinguish new from used electronics, making it difficult to police dumping through second-hand trade.

Market power remains uneven

At sites like Alaba, middlemen can profit substantially while informal collectors face unsafe work and weak bargaining power.

06 / RIGHTS IMPLICATIONS

E-waste is not only an environmental issue. It is also a digital rights issue tied to environmental justice, labour protection, children’s rights, and structural dependency in the global electronics economy.

    lightbulb

    07 / POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS

    The policy response has to move beyond waste management alone into digital policy, import control, labour protection, and public health.

    DIGITAL POLICY

    Integrate e-waste into digital growth planning

    Digital economy policy, broadband plans, and inclusion strategies should all carry e-waste impact assessments rather than treating hardware waste as an afterthought.

    Require every major digital growth framework to account for material waste costs.

    EXTENDED PRODUCER RESPONSIBILITY

    Make EPR enforceable at scale

    Mandatory registration, auditable recycling targets, and penalties for non-compliance are needed if EPR is to move beyond paper architecture.

    Back EPRON and NESREA with enforcement capacity, targets, and public compliance pressure.

    IMPORT CONTROL

    Use the 2025 Basel amendments aggressively

    Nigeria now has stronger legal tools for controlling e-waste shipments, but those tools mean little without inspection capacity and customs training.

    Invest in port-level inspection systems and customs readiness for prior informed consent enforcement.

    INTERNATIONAL ADVOCACY

    Press for transboundary EPR fee transfer

    Producer responsibility fees collected in exporting countries should follow products when they move into Nigerian markets as used electronics.

    Treat lifecycle cost transfer as a core fairness demand in international e-waste diplomacy.

    WORKER PROTECTION

    Formalise the informal sector around rights, not paperwork

    Registration alone will not solve toxic exposure, weak bargaining power, or income insecurity in informal recycling.

    Tie formalisation to labour protections, health coverage, and livable income safeguards.

    PUBLIC HEALTH

    Institutionalise health impact monitoring

    Mortality and groundwater findings require a standing policy response rather than one-off research attention.

    Monitor major sites, remediate contaminated water, and restrict residential expansion around active hotspots.

    09 / CLOSING VIEW

    Nigeria’s e-waste crisis is a direct consequence of rapid digital growth combined with heavy dependence on imported electronics. A sustainable digital future requires control over import flows, formal recovery systems, worker and child protection, and a digital policy framework that accounts for the material costs of connectivity.
